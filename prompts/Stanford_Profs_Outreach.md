A cold-email drafting prompt for reaching out to professors (or any practitioner) whose work sits at the center of the field you're trying to enter. Optimized for *response*, not for *appearing impressive*.

**Sits in the curriculum at:** Session 4 · Lesson 1 (Outreach) · Direct Practice

## The prompt

```
You are a cold-email coach who has gotten responses from 100+
busy academics. Help me draft an outreach email that gets a
real reply (not a polite no-reply).

Here is who I'm writing to:

Name: [Professor's full name]
Institution: [University]
Their work: [2-3 sentences about what they study]
Specific paper / talk / project of theirs I've engaged with: [be specific]
What surprised, confused, or hooked me about it: [be specific]

Here is who I am:

What I'm working on: [1-2 sentences]
Why their work matters for what I'm doing: [1-2 sentences]
What I'm specifically asking for: [must be one concrete ask]
What I'm NOT asking for: [also list — to show respect for their time]

## Draft the email with these constraints

- Subject line: 6 words max. Specific to their work, not generic.
- Opening: One specific sentence proving I read their work. Quote
  a line, name a result, reference a tweet — something that could
  not have been written by anyone else.
- Body: 3-4 short paragraphs. No flattery. No "huge fan." Tell them
  what I'm doing and why their work intersects.
- The ask: ONE concrete request. A 15-minute call. A pointer to
  one paper. An intro to one person. Never "any advice you have."
- Close: An exit ramp. "Totally understand if you're too busy" —
  in your own words.
- No signature beyond name and one link to my work.

## Critique pass

After drafting, audit your own email:
- Could this have been sent to any of their colleagues unchanged? If yes, rewrite.
- Is there a flattery word I could cut? (Cut it.)
- Is the ask concrete enough that they can say yes or no in one sentence?
- Is there a sentence the professor would skim past? (Cut it.)

Give me the final version after critique, plus a 2-line summary
of what you changed and why.
```

## Variant: practitioner DM (X / LinkedIn)

```
Same setup as above, but:
- Compress to 4 sentences total
- No subject line, no signature
- One concrete ask
- Lead with the specific thing they said that I'm responding to
- End with the ask
```

## What separates a reply from a no-reply

The single biggest predictor of a reply is the opening sentence proving you actually read their work. Most cold emails fail here. They open with "I'm a student interested in your research." That sentence could be sent to any of 500 professors. The professor knows it, and archives the email in 0.4 seconds.

Replace it with: *"Your 2023 paper on [exact title] made the claim that [specific result], which I'd been assuming was settled the other way after reading [other person's] work."* — and you get replies.
